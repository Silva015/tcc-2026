\label{chap:results}

The Results chapter is the climax of your TCC. 

Its sole purpose is to present the data collected during your experiments. It is a "just the facts" zone where you demonstrate whether your hypothesis held true under testing.

The results should be organized around your research objectives. If you set out to compare KANs against traditional MLPs, this chapter must provide the direct numerical comparison of those two models.

In a AI TCC, text is often secondary to visual data representation. This chapter consists of:

- Performance Metrics: Tables comparing F1-scores, Precision, and Recall across different wound types (e.g., entrance vs. exit wounds).
- Computational Efficiency: Graphs showing the training loss curves and the time taken per epoch, which is a major selling point for KAN architectures.
- Confusion Matrices: These are vital for forensic applications, as they show exactly which types of wounds the AI is misidentifying.
- Inference Examples: Visual examples of the model’s "predictions" on actual ballistic images, perhaps using heatmaps to show which parts of the wound the KAN layers focused on.


\section{Discussion}

A common mistake in undergraduate theses is mixing results with discussion. To maintain academic rigor, the beggining of Results chapter shows "What happened?" while the this subsection answers "What does it mean?". 

For example, in the previous part of the result chapter, you can state that "The KAN model achieved a XX\% accuracy." In this Discussion subsection, you would say: "The 94\% accuracy suggests that KAN's spline-based activation is superior for capturing the irregular textures of ballistic trauma."
